# Add List with Adjustment Strategy — The Compensation Workout

## In one line
A mental-math game where students nudge two awkward two-digit addends toward a friendly pair (like 60 + 70) using +/- buttons — discovering, round after round, that adding to one number and subtracting the same amount from the other never changes the sum.

## Who it's for
Class 4–5 students (ages ~9–11) who can already add two two-digit numbers on paper but freeze when asked to do it in their head. The game assumes the student knows what addition is; it's pushing them toward a *strategy* — compensation — that turns "ugh, 58 + 72, let me carry the 1" into "easy, 60 + 70, that's 130." It's the move from mechanical addition to flexible mental arithmetic.

## What it tries to teach
The whole game is built around one big idea, the "compensation" or "adjustment" strategy: **what you add to one addend, you can subtract from the other, and the sum stays the same**.
Three threads inside that:
- **Benchmark-number sense.** Students learn to look at any two-digit number and see the nearest "friendly" multiple of 10. `58` *wants* to be `60`; `72` *wants* to be `70`; `66` *wants* to be either `60` or `70` depending on its partner.
- **Conservation of sum.** The deepest lesson hidden in every round: no matter how many times the student adjusts the numbers, the final answer is *always* the same as the original sum. The +/- buttons aren't changing the math — they're changing how the math *feels*. Once a student internalizes this, they have a tool they'll reach for forever.
- **Self-correction.** A Reset button lets students undo any path they've taken. This is small but pedagogically important: students learn that experimentation is free, that wrong paths aren't penalties, and that math is a thing you *play* with.

## What the player sees and does
A clean workspace with two number boxes side by side, separated by a big black `+`. Each box shows one of the addends — say `58` and `72`. Above each box is a small pink `−` button; below each box is a small green `+` button. To the right of the workspace is a `↺ Reset` pill. Below the workspace is an empty `?` input field, and a yellow "Next Round" button.

The student's job is simple: massage the numbers until they look easy, then type in the sum.

- **Tap `+` on the first box** → 58 ticks up to 59.
- **Tap `−` on the second box** → 72 ticks down to 71.
- **A few more taps** → the boxes show `60` and `70`. The student visibly relaxes; this is now a problem they can do in their head.
- **Type `130` in the `?` field, tap "Next Round"** → green tick, a soft *ding*, and the next pair appears. The student didn't compute `58 + 72` directly — they computed `60 + 70`, and the game accepted it.

If they type a wrong sum, the input shakes red, a brief voice-over says *"Not quite — the sum of 58 and 72 is 130"*, the boxes reset to their starting values, the input clears, and they get a fresh try on the same round (but they lose one heart from the three at the top).

There's no penalty for tapping `+` and `−` "too much," and no scoring of how clever the path is. The only thing that's checked is the final typed sum.

## Shape of the experience
9 rounds in three small stages of 3 each:

- **Stage 1 — Easy benchmarks (Rounds 1–3).** Pairs like `58 + 72` and `61 + 69`. Both addends are within ±4 of a multiple of 10, so a couple of taps on each side gets the student to the friendly pair. Most students hit `60 + 70 = 130` and move on quickly.
- **Stage 2 — Tighter adjustments (Rounds 4–6).** Pairs like `62 + 68`. The boxes here are visually different — they get a soft blue border instead of grey — so the student feels the difficulty step up without being told. The benchmarks are still in reach but require thinking about *which* addend to nudge first.
- **Stage 3 — Wider gaps (Rounds 7–9).** Pairs like `66 + 74` where each addend is ±3 to ±5 away from a benchmark. Students have to commit to a target pair (often `70 + 70`) and adjust both sides by larger amounts. This is the stretch zone where the strategy really pays off.

## Win condition and stars
9 rounds, 3 lives. Stars are awarded by **how many rounds the student finished without losing a heart** (i.e. first-try correct):
- **3 stars** — All 9 rounds first-try correct (3 lives intact at the end).
- **2 stars** — At least 7 of 9 rounds first-try correct (1 life lost, possibly 2).
- **1 star** — Finished all 9 rounds with at least 1 life remaining, but with more wrong attempts.
- **0 stars** — Game over (3 wrong attempts before round 9).

Speed isn't graded. The point of this game is *thinking carefully* and watching the strategy click — rushing would defeat the lesson.

## Feel and motivation
- **The +/- buttons feel tactile.** Each tap is a quiet click, no fanfare. Students should feel like they're sliding tiles around, not pressing a quiz button.
- **Reset is friendly, not punitive.** It's drawn as a circular-arrow icon, not a red "ABORT." Students can use it as freely as they want, and the game neither rewards nor penalizes its use.
- **The reveal happens on the student.** The game never explicitly says "this is the compensation strategy" or "watch how the sum stays the same." It lets the student notice. By round 4 or 5, most students have figured out that they can be lazy and just adjust to friendly numbers. That moment of self-discovery is the lesson.
- **Replay practices the move.** A second run loads fresh number pairs from the same difficulty curve, so a student who got 2 stars and tries again is actually rehearsing the strategy on new material.

## Why it works pedagogically
Compensation is one of the highest-leverage mental-math strategies in arithmetic — it shows up again in subtraction (counting up to a friendlier number), in fractions (rewriting `4/5 + 7/10` by adjusting the denominator), and in algebra (moving terms across an equals sign without changing the equation). Most curricula introduce it as a side note in a textbook chapter and assume it'll stick. This game makes the strategy the *only* viable way to play efficiently — you *can* stare at `66 + 74` and try to compute it directly, but every student quickly realizes that nudging to `70 + 70 = 140` is faster, easier, and feels better. The game teaches the strategy by making it the path of least resistance.

A 9-round session takes 4–7 minutes. Short enough for a classroom warm-up; long enough that the "aha" moment arrives within a single sitting.
