# Pot Patterns — The Multiples Hunt

## In one line
A tap-all-that-apply game where the student looks at a 4×5 grid of numbers from 1 to 20, hunts down every multiple of 5 (or 3, or 4) tucked among the rest, and taps each one — without picking up a single non-multiple along the way.

## Who it's for
Class 3–4 students (ages ~8–10) who can chant *"5, 10, 15, 20"* on cue but can't yet *recognize* `15` as a multiple of `5` when it's sitting in a grid next to `13` and `17`. The game targets the specific stuck-point where students know multiples as a chant-list but not yet as a *property of a number* they can spot at a glance. It's the bridge between skip-counting and divisibility.

## What it tries to teach
The whole game is built around one quiet skill: **recognizing multiples of a small number on sight**, in any order, mixed in with non-multiples.
Three threads inside that:
- **Multiple-of-5 fluency.** The launch round is multiples of 5 (`5, 10, 15, 20`). Students learn to scan a grid and pick out *just the numbers ending in 0 or 5* — the visual rule that's true for every multiple of 5 forever. This is the game's gift of a permanent shortcut.
- **Multiples of 2, 3, 4 by skip-count.** Later rounds shift to multiples of 2, 3, 4 — where there's no clean "ends in 0 or 5" trick. The student has to skip-count internally (`3, 6, 9, 12, 15, 18`) and tap each one as it arrives. They build a private mental rhythm for each times-table.
- **What's NOT a multiple.** Just as important as picking the multiples is leaving the rest alone. Tapping `7` when the target is "multiples of 5" loses a heart. The student learns to *resist the temptation to tap a number that just feels math-y* — a small but vital piece of mathematical self-control.

## What the player sees and does
A clean white panel. Top status row — Q1 badge with avatar, heart icon with `2` lives, and `0/10` star tally on the right.

Below the status, a small story sets the scene: *"You just arranged 9 pots such that each horizontal line had 3 pots."* And right under that, a soft grey strip shows three rows of three flowerpots — a visual reminder of what an array of 9 in rows of 3 looks like. This isn't decorative; it primes the student to think about *grouping into equal lines*.

Then the actual prompt: *"Now Priya wants to arrange flower pots in horizontal lines where each line must have **5 pots**. Select all the possible number of pots she can arrange using as many horizontal lines as she wants."* The bold *5 pots* is the magic word — it means "find the multiples of 5."

Below the prompt sits the grid: a 4-row × 5-column block of small dashed-border square tiles, each holding a number from 1 to 20. Tiles are the same size, neutral coloured, and unselected by default.

A **Submit** button sits below the grid (lit only after at least one tile is tapped).

- **Tap a correct multiple** → the tile fills with a soft green and a check appears; a small *click* sound. The tile stays green and tappable (so the student can untap if they change their mind).
- **Tap a non-multiple** → the tile flashes red, shakes, and immediately deselects. A heart shatters. A short hint slides in: *"`7` is not a multiple of 5. Try numbers ending in 0 or 5."*
- **Submit with the right set** → all green tiles pulse, a *cha-ching* plays, the round badge ticks up.
- **Submit with a missing multiple** → the missing tile glows yellow, a heart shatters, and the round ends gently with the full correct set revealed.

## Shape of the experience
10 rounds across three small stages:

- **Stage 1 — Multiples of 5 (Rounds 1–3).** Grid 1–20. Targets: `5, 10, 15, 20`. The "ends in 0 or 5" rule is gentle and obvious; most kids ace it.
- **Stage 2 — Multiples of 2 (Rounds 4–6).** Grid 1–20. Targets: every even number. Ten correct taps in one round — the most of any round in the game. Students learn to scan rapidly.
- **Stage 3 — Multiples of 3 and 4 (Rounds 7–10).** Grid expands to 1–25 or 1–30. Targets are sparser (`3, 6, 9, 12, 15, 18, 21, 24` or `4, 8, 12, 16, 20, 24`). Decoys like `13`, `14`, `19`, `21` are placed adjacent to the true multiples to test for "looks-like-a-multiple" guesses.

Between stages a one-line takeaway slides in: *"Stage 1 done. Multiples of 5 always end in 0 or 5 — that's a rule that never breaks."*

## Win condition and stars
The student starts with 3 hearts, carried across rounds. Stars are awarded **per round**:
- **3 stars** — all correct multiples tapped, zero non-multiples tapped, submitted on the first try.
- **2 stars** — all correct multiples tapped after one wrong tap.
- **1 star** — round completed eventually (right multiples submitted) but with multiple wrong taps.
- **0 stars** — round failed (hearts ran out before submit).

The game ends when either all 10 rounds are complete or all 3 hearts are lost. A perfect run is 30 stars; the headline tally reads *"X / 10"* with later stages weighted higher.

## Feel and motivation
- **The flowerpot framing earns its keep.** The opening pots-in-rows-of-3 array isn't decorative — it's a mini visual proof. The student *sees* that 9 pots make 3 rows of 3, so when the next round asks for "rows of 5" they understand the question concretely. Subsequent rounds drop the pots and stay symbolic, but the meaning carries over.
- **Tile colour is informative.** Green for tapped-correct, red for tapped-wrong, neutral for untapped. No other colours. The student's eye learns to scan for "still-neutral numbers I haven't checked" without confusion.
- **Decoys teach.** Non-multiples placed adjacent to multiples in the grid (`13` next to `12` and `15`, for example) are deliberate. A student who taps too fast based on visual proximity will get caught; a student who *checks divisibility* will not.
- **Untap is forgiveness.** A correctly tapped tile can be untapped before submit. The student can second-guess themselves without losing a heart, which means the lives are spent only on real misjudgements, not on slips.

## Why it works pedagogically
The leap from skip-counting (rote chant) to divisibility (property of a number) is one of the quiet gates of primary arithmetic. A student who has only the chant must mentally skip-count from 0 every time they wonder if `35` is a multiple of `5`; a student who has the *property* glances at `35` and knows. That single shift unlocks fractions (`15/5 = 3` becomes obvious), times-tables (multiples of 3 stop being a list to recite), and later number theory (factors, primes, LCM). This game pushes the student through that gate with the lowest possible cognitive load — a grid of small numbers and a single yes-or-no question — so the move from chant to recognition can happen without being buried under harder math at the same time. A 10-round session takes about 5 minutes; doing it daily for two weeks visibly speeds up a student's recognition of multiples in any later context.
