# Pack or No Pack — The Multiples Reasoning Game

## In one line
A two-step shop game where the student decides whether a target quantity (like *exactly 10 bottles*) can be bought from a shop that only sells in fixed packs (like *packs of 6*), and then types **why** in plain words — turning a yes/no gut feeling into a real explanation about multiples.

## Who it's for
Class 3–5 students (ages ~8–11) who have just met the words *multiple*, *factor*, and *pack of n* and can recite the 6-times table, but freeze when a word problem dresses the same idea up in shop language. The game is for the student who can answer "is 12 a multiple of 6?" on a quiz but pauses at *"can you buy exactly 10 from a pack-of-6 shop?"* — same maths, different costume.

## What it tries to teach
The whole game is built around one quiet move: **recognising that "buyable in packs of n" and "is a multiple of n" are the same question**, and being able to *say* so.
Three threads inside that:
- **Translating shop words to maths.** *Packs of 6* → multiples of 6. *Exactly 10* → *is 10 in the 6-times table?* The student rehearses this swap until it's automatic.
- **Justifying with a number, not a vibe.** A correct *No* with the explanation *"6 is too small and 12 is too big — there's no multiple of 6 equal to 10"* is worth more than a correct *No* with *"because"*. The text input forces the student to *show* the multiple, not just feel the answer.
- **Spotting the nearest neighbours.** The strongest justifications name the multiples that *bracket* 10 — *6 and 12*. That habit (find the closest multiples on either side) is the same move used later for division-with-remainder and rounding.

## What the player sees and does
A clean white panel, very minimal. Top row: a small avatar with the round badge `Q1`, a heart counter (lives left), a `0/10` star tally with a yellow star.

The body opens with a single bold line — *"In a shop, water bottles are sold in packs of 6."* Below that, a chunky cartoon image of six bottles bound together in a single pack, drawn in friendly cyan and royal blue — the visual anchors the abstract idea of "a pack" so the student can *see* the unit.

Underneath the picture, the question lands: *"Will you be able to buy exactly 10 bottles from this shop?"*

Two large stacked answer tiles sit centre-screen: **Yes** on top, **No** below. They're plain, evenly sized, and inviting.

Beneath the tiles, a small label reads *"Explain your above answer."* and a single-line text input opens up. The keyboard slides up. The student types a short sentence — full keyboard, free typing, no template.

- **Tap the right option then submit a justification containing a valid multiple of 6** (e.g. *"because 6 and 12 are multiples of 6 but 10 is not"*) → both turn green, a chime plays, the round resolves with full marks.
- **Tap the right option but type a vague justification** (just *"because"* or *"yes"* or random text with no number) → the option stays green but the text box turns amber and a soft prompt appears: *"Tell me a multiple of 6 to back that up."* The student edits in place. No life lost.
- **Tap the wrong option** → the tile shakes red, a heart shatters, the correct tile pulses green, and a one-line hint surfaces: *"6 × 1 = 6, 6 × 2 = 12. Where would 10 fit?"* The round ends.

## Shape of the experience
10 rounds, scenarios stay shop-flavoured but the maths drifts:

- **Rounds 1–3 — Obvious nos.** Packs of 6, target 10. Packs of 4, target 7. Packs of 5, target 12. The targets sit clearly between two multiples — the student just has to name them.
- **Rounds 4–7 — Mixed yes/no.** Some targets *are* multiples (packs of 4, target 16 → yes, *4 × 4 = 16*). The student has to actually check, and the justification asks for the multiplication that proves it.
- **Rounds 8–10 — Bigger numbers, sneakier targets.** Packs of 7 with target 49 (yes), packs of 8 with target 50 (no, brackets are 48 and 56), packs of 12 with target 60 (yes). Numbers leave the times-table comfort zone, so students must actually count up rather than recall.

A two-second toast surfaces between rounds: *"Nice — 48 and 56 are the closest multiples, 50 sits between them."*

## Win condition and stars
Three lives carry across all 10 rounds.

- **3 stars** — finish all 10 rounds with all 3 lives intact.
- **2 stars** — finish all 10 with 1 or 2 lives lost.
- **1 star** — finish all 10 with all lives gone is impossible; instead, 1 star is awarded for finishing 7+ rounds before running out of lives.
- **0 stars** — fewer than 7 rounds completed.

The justification step never costs a life on its own — it only ever asks for a do-over with a hint. Lives are spent on the binary choice, where the real reasoning lives.

## Feel and motivation
- **Pictures, not symbols.** Every round shows a real-world pack — bottles, eggs in cartons, biscuits in sleeves. The visual carries the multiplicative structure so the student doesn't have to abstract it twice.
- **Free-text justification, gently judged.** The game checks that the student's typed explanation contains *some* relevant multiple of the pack size. It doesn't require a specific phrase. A child who writes *"12 is in the table but 10 isn't"* gets the same green tick as one who writes *"6 × 1 = 6, 6 × 2 = 12, no 10"*. The flexibility matters — kids explain things in their own voices.
- **The keyboard is the lesson.** Forcing the student to *type a number* is the whole point: *because* alone is not maths. Once they're typing 6 or 12 or 18, they're handling multiples whether they realise it or not.

## Why it works pedagogically
Multiples are usually drilled as a list (*6, 12, 18, 24...*) and tested by multiple choice — both formats let a student pass without ever using the concept. This game pulls multiples out of the recitation context and into a tiny shop-keeping decision, then makes the student *say the multiples out loud* (well, type them) as evidence. That is the move teachers actually want: *I know it's not a multiple because here are the two multiples on either side of it.* Once a child has produced that sentence twenty times in different shop scenarios, the same reasoning powers their later work on factors, division remainders, and lowest common multiples — all of which are just dressed-up versions of *find the nearest multiple of n*. A 10-round session takes about 6–9 minutes. Long enough to drill the pattern, short enough to slot in as a warm-up.
