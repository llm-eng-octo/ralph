# Pack the Bears — The Equal-Sharing Reasoner

## In one line
A two-step packing puzzle where the student first decides *whether* a pile of teddy bears can be split evenly into a given number of boxes, and then types *how many* go in each box — turning division from a pure calculation into a yes-or-no judgement followed by a quotient.

## Who it's for
Class 2–3 students (ages ~7–9) who are just meeting the idea of division as equal sharing. They can count, they can group, they may even know "9 divided by 3 is 3" if asked directly — but they haven't yet wired the *judgement* part: looking at 9 things and 4 boxes and instantly knowing "no, that won't share evenly." This game targets the moment when sharing is still concrete (real bears, real boxes) and students need to learn to *check* before they divide.

## What it tries to teach
The whole game is built around one chunky skill: **deciding whether a total can be split into equal groups, and naming the size of each group when it can.**
Three threads inside that:
- **Divisibility intuition.** When a child stares at "9 bears, 9 boxes" they need to recognise the answer is *yes, one each*. When they see "9 bears, 4 boxes" they need to recognise *no*, without having to physically deal out 9 bears. The game alternates yes-cases and no-cases so the student practises both responses.
- **Quotient as group size.** The second input asks "how many in each box?" — explicitly labelling the quotient as the *size of one group*, not just an answer to a sum. This re-grounds division in its sharing meaning.
- **Reading a real situation.** The instruction is wrapped in a friendly story ("good job packing 9 teddy bears in 3 boxes…") — students learn to lift the relevant numbers out of a sentence rather than have them pre-extracted.

## What the player sees and does
A clean white panel. At the very top, a thin status row — student avatar and round badge on the left, hearts and a star tally on the right. Below that, two warm sentences of context in friendly text, then a row of cardboard boxes (Box 1 through Box 9) sitting above a cluster of cartoon teddy bears. The visual is the heart of the screen — children can *see* the 9 bears and the 9 boxes and intuit the answer before they touch anything.

Below the picture, the first question appears: *"Can 9 boxes be used?"* with a small dropdown reading "Yes/No". Once the student answers correctly, a second question slides in: *"If 9 boxes are used, how many bears are in each box?"* with three small input boxes laid out in a tiny equation form (`9 ÷ 9 = ___`).

- **Right at step 1 (yes/no)** → the dropdown locks green with a soft chime, and the second question reveals.
- **Right at step 2 (quotient)** → green tick on the equation, the round resolves, star count ticks up.
- **Wrong on the dropdown** → the dropdown flashes red, a heart shatters, and the question stays open for a retry. The image of bears subtly highlights how they'd group — *"can you make 9 even piles from these 9 bears? try again."*
- **Wrong on the quotient** → the input shakes, a heart shatters, and the correct number is *not* given away — the student tries again until they enter the right value.

## Shape of the experience
10 rounds, with the bear/box numbers shifting each time:

- **Rounds 1–3 — Obvious yes-cases.** 9 bears into 9 boxes (1 each), 8 bears into 4 boxes (2 each), 12 bears into 6 boxes (2 each). Confidence builders. The student says yes and types a small quotient.
- **Rounds 4–6 — Obvious no-cases.** 9 bears into 4 boxes, 7 bears into 3 boxes, 10 bears into 6 boxes. The dropdown answer is "No"; the second question doesn't appear. Students learn that *not every total can be shared evenly*.
- **Rounds 7–10 — Mixed and slightly larger.** 12 into 3, 15 into 4, 18 into 6, 20 into 5. The student must decide each time whether to expect a clean division.

## Win condition and stars
Three lives across the whole 10-round session.
- **3 stars** — finish all 10 rounds with at least 8 first-tap correct decisions (across 18 step-decisions: 10 yes/no answers + up to 8 quotients).
- **2 stars** — finish all 10 rounds with 5–7 first-tap correct.
- **1 star** — finish all 10 rounds with fewer than 5 first-tap correct, or finish with hearts to spare but many retries.
- **0 stars** — all three hearts lost before round 10.

## Feel and motivation
- **Bears, not numbers.** The image is doing the math teaching, not the digits. A child who's stuck can count bears, point to boxes, and physically work it out — this is deliberate at this age.
- **Two-step, not one.** Most worksheet division asks for a quotient straight away. This game refuses to give the second input until the yes/no judgement is right. That trains the *check first* habit.
- **Friendly numbers only.** Totals stay under 25, divisors under 10. Quotients are always single digits. Arithmetic should never be the obstacle when the lesson is about divisibility.

## Why it works pedagogically
Long before students meet long division, they need a feel for which numbers split evenly and which don't. This game makes that intuition visible by separating the *can-this-share* decision from the *how-many-each* calculation. Students leave with a foundation move that pays off years later when they meet factors, primes, and remainders — the question "is this divisible?" stops being abstract because they've already answered it dozens of times with bears and boxes.

A 10-round session takes 4–6 minutes — perfect as a five-minute starter for a primary class, or a quick check-in before a lesson on remainders.
