# Whose Strategy? — The Estimation Decision Game

## In one line
A short, video-led estimation game where the student watches two characters explain *different ways* of guessing the total water in two jars, and then commits to whichever strategy they trust more before the answer is revealed.

## Who it's for
Class 3–5 students (ages ~8–11) who can compute exact answers in tidy textbook problems but freeze in the face of *real-world* quantities — the messy ones with no labels, no rulers, just a picture of a jar and a question. The game is for the moment a child needs to learn that *estimation is a real strategy with rules of its own*, not just "guessing because you don't know how to compute."

## What it tries to teach
The whole game is built around one habit that classroom math is bad at teaching: **picking an estimation strategy and committing to it before peeking at the answer.**
Three threads inside that:
- **There are different valid estimation strategies.** One character (Ravi) might use a *unit-comparison* strategy — *"Jar 1 looks like four glasses, Jar 2 looks like five glasses, each glass is about 200 ml, so the total is about 1.8 litres."* Another character (Sam) might use a *bracket-and-narrow* strategy — *"both jars together are clearly more than 1 litre and less than 2 litres; let's start at 1.5 and adjust."* Both can land near the right answer; both are valid; the student has to consider *the strategy itself* as the unit of choice.
- **Strategy choice precedes computation.** Most kids, faced with this problem, jump straight to numbers. The game refuses to let them. The choice of *strategy* must be committed to via a button press *before* any computation reward is given. This is unusual classroom hygiene and a very high-leverage habit for word problems and competition math.
- **Confidence under uncertainty.** The point is not to land on the exact correct millilitre count — it is to *commit* to an approach. Sam's strategy might give 1.6 litres; Ravi's might give 1.7. The student isn't punished for picking either, but they are nudged to notice *why* one feels more reliable to them in this scenario.

## What the player sees and does
A clean, calm panel — almost more like a small slide than a math puzzle. The status row at the top is minimal: round badge `Q1` on the left, progress chip `0/10` with a yellow star on the right. (No timer; this is a thinking exercise, not a sprint.)

Below the status row, the framing question:

> *"Ravi and Sam use different strategies to guess the total amount of water in the two jars. Whose strategy would you like to use?"*

In the centre of the screen, an illustration of two glass jars side by side, each filled with light-blue water to slightly different levels, labelled *"Jar 1"* and *"Jar 2"* underneath. The jars are clean line-art — no ml markings, no rulers, just the jars and their water levels. This is a deliberate choice: the puzzle is about *how* you'd estimate, not how precisely you can read a measurement.

Below the jars, an embedded video player — typically a 60–90 second clip — in which both characters explain their strategies in turn. The player has standard controls: play/pause, scrub, mute, fullscreen, settings.

Below the video, two strategy buttons:

- **Ravi's strategy** (a soft pill button)
- **Sam's strategy** (another pill button)

At the very bottom, a wide yellow **Submit & check** button, sized large like a real call-to-action. This button is greyed out until exactly one of the two strategy buttons has been selected.

The student plays in this order:

1. **Reads the question** at the top.
2. **Looks at the jars** to ground their intuition about the two volumes.
3. **Plays the video** and listens to both strategies — pausing, replaying, or skipping as they wish.
4. **Selects one of the two strategies** by tapping its button. The tapped button highlights; the other dims; either can be re-selected.
5. **Taps Submit & check.** The game now shows the *outcome* — what each strategy yielded numerically, what the actual total was, and how close each strategy got. The student sees whether their committed strategy was the more accurate one for *this specific scenario*.

Behaviours:

- **Submit with a strategy selected** → the screen reveals both strategies' computed estimates side by side, the true volume below, and a one-line takeaway like *"Sam's bracket-and-narrow worked better here because both jars were close to standard glass size."* No life is lost regardless of which strategy was picked, because the act of committing is the lesson.
- **Try to submit without selecting** → the Submit button stays inert, with a soft prompt *"choose a strategy first."*
- **Replay video any time** → uninterrupted; the video is a reference, not a one-shot.

## Shape of the experience
10 rounds, each a short scenario built around two characters and a different estimation context:

- **Rounds 1–3 — Volume scenarios (jars, glasses, bottles).** Strategies revolve around *comparing to a known unit volume*. Visually similar to the reference round.
- **Rounds 4–7 — Length and area scenarios.** Two pencils of different lengths; two photo-frames of different shapes; two ribbons. Strategies shift to *unit-counting along a side* versus *gridding-and-counting*.
- **Rounds 8–10 — Crowd, weight, and money scenarios.** A jar of marbles; a bag of grains; a stack of coins. Strategies become *sampling a chunk and multiplying* versus *bracketing a total range*. The math gets less tidy and the strategy choice gets harder.

## Win condition and stars
There are no lives in this game; choosing a strategy is never wrong. Stars are awarded by **how often the student picked the more-accurate strategy across the 10 rounds**:
- **3 stars** — picked the better strategy on at least 8 of 10 rounds.
- **2 stars** — picked the better strategy on 5–7 rounds.
- **1 star** — completed all 10 rounds.
- **0 stars** — abandoned before round 10.

The intent is honest: the game is teaching judgement under uncertainty, and judgement is graded by the rate at which it tracks reality, not by all-or-nothing penalties.

## Feel and motivation
- **The video is the heart.** Each scenario's video is short, warmly narrated, and *acts out* the two strategies with on-screen voice and small annotations. Children who learn best through hearing-and-watching get a route in that pure-text estimation problems would deny them.
- **No tally of money, no scoreboard.** The game's UI is unusually quiet — no big timers, no score combo meters. This signals that estimation is a *quiet*, reflective skill, not a reflex sport. The deliberate calm is the feel.
- **The reveal is generous.** When the student commits, the game does not say *"you were wrong."* It shows both strategies' results on a number line next to the true value, so the student can *see* how close each landed. The student's chosen strategy is highlighted in their colour. The reveal feels like a teacher walking through both methods at the board, not a judgement.

## Why it works pedagogically
Estimation is one of the most under-taught skills in Indian primary math, partly because textbooks reward exact answers and partly because there's no clean way to *grade* an estimate as right or wrong on a worksheet. The result is that students grow up seeing math as either *exactly correct* or *failure*, which makes them allergic to real-world quantities (where exact computation is rarely possible without measurement) and to competition math (where smart bounding is half the game). By presenting the choice of *strategy* as the assessable moment, this game makes estimation feel like a *decision* the student is good or bad at — which, in fact, is what estimation is. The video format also lets the game introduce strategies in a teacher-like voice, which classroom interactive content often misses. A 10-round session takes about 12–18 minutes (videos take time), so this is best used as a once-a-week activity or as a centre-piece lesson during a chapter on measurement and approximation.
