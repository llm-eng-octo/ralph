# Near Doubles — The Drag-One-Then-Adjust Game

## In one line
A drag-and-drop mental-math game where the student is handed two known doubles (`Double of 7 is 14`, `Double of 8 is 16`) and a small pool of `+1` / `-1` adjuster tiles, and must build the sum `7 + 8` by dragging *one* double card and *one* adjuster into a single drop zone above.

## Who it's for
Class 2–3 students (ages ~7–9) who have memorized their doubles (`6+6=12`, `7+7=14`, `8+8=16`) but freeze on near-doubles like `7+8` because they think it's a brand-new fact to learn. The game targets the very specific stuck-point where a student has the doubles in their pocket but hasn't realized they can *use* them as a stepping stone to neighbouring sums. It's the bridge from rote doubles to flexible mental addition.

## What it tries to teach
The whole game is built around one quiet, high-leverage move: **using a known double to compute an unknown near-double by adjusting up or down by one**.
Three threads inside that:
- **Pick the closer double.** For `7 + 8`, both `Double of 7 = 14` and `Double of 8 = 16` are right — `14 + 1` and `16 - 1` both give `15`. The game accepts both, so the student learns that *either* anchor works. This builds flexibility.
- **+1 vs. -1 reasoning.** The crucial decision is whether to *add* or *subtract* the adjuster. If you start from `Double of 7 = 14` and you want `7 + 8`, you need *one more* (because you swapped a 7 for an 8). If you start from `Double of 8 = 16`, you need *one fewer*. The game grades this directly.
- **Doubles as building blocks.** Every round secretly reinforces a double-fact (`7+7`, `8+8`, `9+9`, etc.), because the student has to *use* the double to solve the round. A child who plays this game daily is doing a stealth doubles drill while learning near-doubles.

## What the player sees and does
A clean white panel. Top status row — Q1 badge with avatar, heart icon with `2` lives, `0/10` star tally on the right.

Below it, the prompt in large friendly text: *"You are given the **doubles of 7 and 8**. Find the **sum of 7 and 8** by **placing the tags** in the given area."* The bold phrases pull the eye to the three pieces of the puzzle.

Then a clue panel — two short lines, centred:
- *"Double of 7 is **14**"*
- *"Double of 8 is **16**"*

Below the clue, a hint sentence with a yellow finger pointing down: *"Find sum of **7 and 8** by placing tags here ↓"*

Then the **drop zone**: a single empty rounded rectangle, lightly outlined, sitting on its own.

Below the drop zone, a soft grey panel — the **tag pool** — containing five draggable square tags arranged in two rows: top row `12`, `14`, `16`, `-1`; bottom row a single `+1` tag. The tags are clearly square cards with a soft shadow, the kind that *look* draggable.

The student's job is to drag two tags into the drop zone — one double, one adjuster — that combine to `15`. Multiple correct combinations exist: `14` + `+1`, or `16` + `-1`. Decoy tag `12` (the double of 6) is there to catch students who grab the wrong double. The drop zone is a single slot but accepts up to two tags, evaluating their sum on submit.

- **Drop the right pair (sum = 15)** → both tags pulse green, a quick *chime*, the round badge ticks up, and the next round loads.
- **Drop a pair that sums to anything else** → both tags shake red, return to the pool, a heart shatters, and a one-line hint appears: *"You picked `12` (double of 6). The doubles of **7 and 8** are 14 and 16."*
- **Drop a single tag and submit** → a soft prompt: *"You'll need two tags — a double, and a +1 or -1 to adjust."*

There's no number pad, no typing — the entire interaction is "look at the clues, pick two cards, drop them in the box."

## Shape of the experience
10 rounds, each a near-double sum:

- **Rounds 1–3 — Small near-doubles.** `4 + 5`, `5 + 6`, `6 + 7`. Doubles known, answers ≤ 13. Students get the rhythm.
- **Rounds 4–7 — Tens crossings.** `7 + 8`, `8 + 9`, `9 + 10`. Answers 15–19, which is the cognitively trickiest band for early addition because of the cross-over of ten.
- **Rounds 8–10 — Bigger near-doubles.** `11 + 12`, `12 + 13`, `13 + 14`. Same move, larger numbers — students see that the strategy scales.

Across all 10 rounds the tag pool always offers three doubles (the right one, the right one's neighbour, and one decoy double from a totally different round) plus `+1` and `-1`. Same shape, different numbers.

## Win condition and stars
The student starts with 3 hearts, carried across rounds. Stars are awarded **per round**:
- **3 stars** — correct pair dropped on the first attempt, no decoys touched.
- **2 stars** — correct pair dropped after picking up one wrong tag (returned without dropping it).
- **1 star** — correct pair dropped after a wrong drop.
- **0 stars** — round failed (hearts gone).

The game ends when either all 10 rounds are complete or all 3 hearts are lost. A perfect run is 30 stars, displayed as *"X / 10"* with later rounds weighted higher.

## Feel and motivation
- **Cards feel physical.** Each tag has a real card-shadow and lifts slightly under the cursor. Dragging is satisfying — the card follows the finger smoothly, and snapping into the drop zone makes a small *thunk*. The game is fundamentally about *combining* two pieces, and the tactile feel reinforces that.
- **Two correct paths is a feature.** Either `14 + (+1)` or `16 + (-1)` is accepted. The game never says *"actually you should have used the other double"* — both are equally praised. This is deliberate: the lesson is that *any* nearby double works, and the student gets to pick.
- **The decoy is the previous round's answer.** When the round is `7 + 8`, the decoy tag is `12` (double of 6). When the round is `8 + 9`, the decoy is `14` (double of 7). This is intentional: students who blur recent rounds together will reach for yesterday's number.

## Why it works pedagogically
Mental addition past the doubles is where most early-arithmetic students stall, because schools teach the doubles as a memorized list and then expect kids to magically generalize. They don't. They keep counting on fingers for `7 + 8`. This game forces the *bridge move* — "I know `7 + 7`, so `7 + 8` is one more" — to be the *only* available action. The student literally cannot solve the round without making that move. After 10 rounds the move becomes a habit; after a week of daily play it becomes invisible. The student stops counting fingers for near-doubles, and that frees up their working memory to handle bigger arithmetic without arithmetic itself becoming the obstacle. A session takes about 4 minutes — a perfect five-minutes-before-class warm-up.
