# True or False — The Two-Choice Math Reasoning Sprint

## In one line
A focused single-question game where the student reads a math statement, weighs exactly two answers — *yes / no, true / false, correct / not-yet* — and only commits once they've thought it through, by tapping a deliberate **Submit & check** button.

## Who it's for
Class 4–7 students (ages ~9–13) who are confident enough with the underlying math but tend to *guess fast* on multiple-choice questions. The game is for the student who reads the question, picks the first option that sounds right, and moves on without checking. Two options is the smallest possible MCQ — the entire game is about killing the gap between *gut* and *check*.

## What it tries to teach
The whole game is built around one quiet skill: **slowing down long enough to verify your own answer**. The math itself is secondary; the pause is the lesson.
Three threads inside that:
- **Read the whole prompt.** With only two options on the screen, students who skim usually still get it half-right, which is worse than getting it wrong — they never learn to read carefully. The decoys are written so a careless reader will misjudge them confidently.
- **Mentally test the option.** Before tapping Submit, students should be able to say *why* their pick is right — substitute back, restate in their own words, draw a quick mental picture. The Submit gate gives that thought a few seconds of room to happen.
- **Commit and own it.** Tapping Submit is a tiny ritual of confidence. Students who treat it as a checkpoint rather than a formality stop drifting through MCQ batteries on autopilot.

## What the player sees and does
A clean white panel. At the very top, a thin status row — a small avatar, the round badge ("Q1"), and a star tally on the right (`0/10`) with a friendly star icon. There is **no timer** here; this game is about deliberation.

Below the header, the question prompt sits in calm dark text — usually one or two sentences, never a wall. A typical prompt: *"Is 48 a multiple of 6?"* or *"Does 0.7 round up to 1?"*

Centered below the prompt, exactly **two answer tiles** stack vertically. Each is a roomy outlined rectangle with the option text inside (*"Yes, 48 = 6 × 8"* / *"No, 48 leaves a remainder"*). The tiles are not buttons that fire on tap — tapping one *selects* it (a thin border highlight), and the student can switch their selection as many times as they like before submitting.

A bright **Submit & check** button sits anchored at the bottom of the screen, big and yellow and unmistakable. It's intentionally far from the options, so the student's thumb has to make a deliberate journey.

- **Tap an option** → the tile highlights, Submit lights up.
- **Tap the other option** → the previous selection clears, the new one highlights. Free to flip-flop.
- **Tap Submit with the right option** → the chosen tile turns green, a soft chime plays, the star tally bumps, and the round resolves cleanly.
- **Tap Submit with the wrong option** → the chosen tile turns red, the correct tile glows green for a beat, a heart shatters, and a one-line explanation slides in: *"48 ÷ 6 = 8 exactly — so 48 IS a multiple of 6."*
- **Tap Submit with nothing selected** → a gentle wobble; nothing else happens.

## Shape of the experience
10 rounds, all sharing the same form but escalating in subtlety:

- **Rounds 1–3 — Obvious.** Statements where the right answer is unambiguous if you read it: *"Is 7 an odd number?"*, *"Is 3 + 4 = 8?"* These build the rhythm of read-think-submit.
- **Rounds 4–7 — Trick-prone.** Statements engineered around the most common misreading: *"Is the perimeter of a 3×3 square 9?"* (no — that's the area), *"Is 0.5 less than 0.05?"* (no — students confuse digit count with size). The two options are written so a skim reads as the wrong one.
- **Rounds 8–10 — Edge cases.** Statements that hinge on a *definition* the student should know but rarely thinks about: *"Is 1 a prime number?"*, *"Is zero a multiple of every number?"* These reward genuine understanding over pattern-matching.

There is no time pressure between rounds; the student moves on by tapping a small **Next** chevron after each result.

## Win condition and stars
Three lives carry across all 10 rounds. Stars are awarded on the final tally:
- **3 stars** — 9 or 10 correct on first submit, no lives lost.
- **2 stars** — 7–8 correct, at most one life lost.
- **1 star** — finished all 10 rounds, more than one life lost but at least one heart still alive.
- **0 stars** — all 3 hearts lost before round 10.

The game ends early on third heart loss; otherwise it runs the full 10 rounds.

## Feel and motivation
- **The Submit button is the whole game.** Visually it dominates — bigger than the options, brightly coloured, anchored at the bottom. The message to the student is: *the answer isn't the tap on the option, it's the tap on this button.* That tiny delay is where learning happens.
- **No timer, ever.** The deliberate absence is the point. Two-option questions are easy to rush; the game refuses to reward speed and instead rewards thinking.
- **Decoys are sentences, not single words.** Each option is written as *"Yes, because..."* or *"No, because..."* — so picking the wrong option means committing to a wrong reason, which is harder to do casually than picking a wrong word.

## Why it works pedagogically
Multiple-choice is where careful students and careless students score the same on easy items but diverge sharply on subtle ones. The two-option format strips MCQ down to its essence — there is no elimination strategy, no "well, A and C look similar so it must be B". The student has to actually decide. By forcing a second action (Submit) between the choice and the check, the game inserts a beat for self-verification that doesn't exist in tap-to-answer formats. Done across enough sessions, that beat becomes a habit, and the habit transfers to every other test the student will ever take. A 10-round session lasts 3–5 minutes — short enough to slot in as a daily warm-up before the harder work begins.
