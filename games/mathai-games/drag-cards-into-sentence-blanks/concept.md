# Fill the Sentence — The Math-Vocabulary Cloze Game

## In one line
A drag-and-drop cloze game where the student looks at a worked-out math fact (a long division, a number line, a fraction picture) and finishes a sentence about it — *"3 is __ of 12 and 12 is __ of 3"* — by dragging the right vocabulary word into each blank.

## Who it's for
Class 4–6 students (ages ~9–12) who can *do* the calculation — they can divide 12 by 3 and tell you it works out evenly — but who muddle up the words *factor* and *multiple*, *numerator* and *denominator*, *prime* and *composite* the moment they have to talk about what they did. The game is for the student whose math is correct but whose vocabulary is shaky, and for whom that gap is starting to cost marks on word problems and conceptual questions.

## What it tries to teach
The whole game is built around one specific skill: **mapping a worked math fact to the precise vocabulary that describes it.**
Three threads inside that:
- **Direction matters.** *"3 is a factor of 12"* and *"12 is a multiple of 3"* describe the same division but from opposite sides. Students learn that the smaller number is the factor and the bigger one is the multiple, and the sentence frame makes that direction explicit.
- **Definitions, applied.** Knowing the definition of *factor* in a textbook is one thing; spotting it in an actual division with a remainder of zero is another. The game presents a worked example *first*, then asks the student to label what they're looking at — the order is deliberate.
- **Reading vocabulary actively.** With only two or three words in the pool and two or three blanks in the sentence, the student must consider each word against each blank — they can't just cycle through guesses.

## What the player sees and does
A clean white panel. The familiar status row at the top — a small avatar tile, the round badge ("Q1"), and on the right a heart counter (`2`) and the rolling star tally (`0/10`) with a yellow star.

Below the header, the **prompt sentence** in calm dark text — about one line: *"Use the division below to complete the sentence that explains how 12 and 3 are related."*

The middle of the screen is the **worked math** — a clean visual of the fact the student is about to label. In the canonical layout, this is a long-division frame: a `3` outside the bracket, `12` inside, a `4` written above as the quotient, the subtraction `12 − 12 = 0` shown beneath, and the remainder `0` in dark text. The visual is purple-bordered and orderly, like a teacher's whiteboard.

Beneath the visual, a one-line summary in bold: *"The remainder of the division is **ZERO**,"* — this is the bridge between the visual and the sentence.

Then the **sentence with blanks** itself, broken across two short lines:

> *So, 3 is* `[ blank ]` *of 12 and 12 is*
> `[ blank ]` *of 3.*

The blanks are visible empty boxes the size of the option cards.

At the bottom sits the **answer pool** — a soft grey panel containing two (or three, with a decoy) draggable word cards: `a multiple` and `a factor`. Each card is a clean rectangle with the phrase in dark text.

- **Drag a card into a correct blank** → the card snaps into place, the blank lights briefly in green, the card is removed from the pool.
- **Drag a card into a wrong blank** → a soft bounce rejects the placement and the card returns to the pool with a one-line nudge: *"3 is the divisor — it goes **into** 12 evenly. So 3 is a *factor* of 12."*
- **Both blanks filled correctly** → the whole sentence highlights green, a chime plays, the star tally bumps, and Next Round becomes available.
- **Card placed in wrong blank, three times** → a heart shatters, the correct cards animate into place, and the round ends.

## Shape of the experience
10 rounds across three thematic pockets, drawing on a small, deliberately-narrow vocabulary:

- **Rounds 1–4 — Factors and multiples.** Worked divisions with zero remainder; sentence pairs about *factor* / *multiple*. Numbers stay small (12 ÷ 3, 18 ÷ 6, 25 ÷ 5).
- **Rounds 5–7 — Fraction parts.** A picture of a fraction (3 of 4 boxes shaded); sentence: *"In ¾, 3 is the __ and 4 is the __."* with `numerator` / `denominator` in the pool. Sometimes a third decoy: `whole`.
- **Rounds 8–10 — Number kinds.** A worked check on a number's divisors; sentence: *"7 has only __ factors, so 7 is a __ number."* with `two`, `three`, `prime`, `composite` in the pool.

Three hearts carry across the whole session. There is no per-round timer — this game is about reading carefully.

## Win condition and stars
Stars are awarded across the 10 rounds:
- **3 stars** — at least 9 of 10 rounds completed with no wrong drops.
- **2 stars** — completed all 10 rounds, with up to 4 wrong drops total.
- **1 star** — completed all 10 rounds, more than 4 wrong drops.
- **0 stars** — all 3 hearts lost before round 10.

## Feel and motivation
- **The visual leads, the sentence follows.** Putting the worked division (or fraction picture, or factor list) at the top of the screen and the sentence below it teaches a habit: *look at the math first, then describe it.* Most textbooks invert this — they give the definition first, then ask the student to apply it — which is exactly backwards from how vocabulary actually sticks.
- **The pool is tiny.** Two cards for two blanks (or three for three) means there is no busywork of scanning a long list. The student's whole attention goes to *which word means which*, not *which words exist*.
- **Wrong drops teach with one line.** Hint feedback is always one sentence and always grounded in the visual: *"3 goes into 12 evenly, so 3 is a factor."* Never abstract.

## Why it works pedagogically
Math vocabulary is where careful students lose marks they shouldn't. *Multiple* and *factor* are not difficult ideas — they are difficult *words*, and the difficulty is purely linguistic. By anchoring each vocabulary card to a worked example the student can see, this game turns the labels from arbitrary jargon into descriptions of something on the screen. The sentence-frame format also doubles as practice for how these terms appear in word problems on tests, where the student doesn't get to compute first and label later — they have to read the label and decode the math behind it. A 10-round session is 4–6 minutes; running it after a fresh lesson on factors, fractions, or primes locks the labels onto the concept while it's still warm.
