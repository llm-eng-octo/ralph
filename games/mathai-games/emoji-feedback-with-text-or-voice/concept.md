# Mood Check — The End-of-Activity Reflection Moment

## In one line
A short, warm reflection prompt where the student picks the emoji that matches how an activity *felt* and then explains it in a sentence — typed or spoken — so a teacher can see not just whether the math landed but whether the child enjoyed it landing.

## Who it's for
Students of any age (Class 2 onwards), but especially Class 2–6 children at the end of a math worksheet, lesson, or game session. It is not a math task; it is the small, structured pause that comes *right after* one. The specific stuck-point is the wall most kids hit when an adult asks "did you like that?" and they shrug — unsure how to answer, unsure if "no" is allowed, unsure how to be specific. Four labelled emojis lower the bar from "compose a sentence" to "tap a face."

## What it tries to teach
This screen is teaching a meta-skill, not a math skill. The leverage point is **honest, low-friction self-assessment** — the habit of noticing how a learning moment landed and saying so out loud. Three small threads:
- **Naming a feeling.** Each emoji is paired with a short caption — *"I got bored"*, *"I needed more help"*, *"It was tough, but I liked it"*, *"I had a lot of fun!"* — so the child connects the picture to a sentence they could say themselves. Over time, the captions become the child's own internal vocabulary.
- **Separating "hard" from "bad."** The third option, *"It was tough, but I liked it"*, is the most important tile on the screen. It lets a child say *"this was difficult and I'm okay with that"* — a sentiment most worksheets don't even have a button for.
- **Voice as a real input.** The follow-up textbox accepts typed words *or* a tap-to-record audio reply. For a child who freezes at a blank textbox, the microphone is the difference between feedback and silence.

## What the player sees and does
A spacious, mostly-white screen. A small avatar and the question label *"Q1"* sit at the top-left; a star tally (`0/10`) sits at the top-right. The body of the screen is laid out in a clean vertical stack with lots of breathing room — there is no grid, no timer, no pressure.

A single warm sentence at the top: *"I'd love to know how you felt about this activity!"* Below it, the prompt *"Tap an emoji that shows how you feel"* with a finger-pointing emoji guiding the eye downward.

Then the heart of the screen: a row of **four large round emoji buttons** evenly spaced — sleeping face, sad/teary face, sweat-smile, heart-eyes — each with its caption stacked underneath. The buttons are big enough for a six-year-old to thumb-tap on a phone, with no neighbours close enough to mis-tap.

Below that, a follow-up prompt: *"Can you tell me why you selected the above emoji?"* and *"Please share more details here"*. A wide rounded text area sits underneath, with a microphone icon and a keyboard icon in the bottom-left corner — the child picks their input mode. An undo arrow sits in the bottom-right of the input box.

- **Tap an emoji** → the chosen face lights up in colour while the others fade to grey, a soft confirmation chime plays, and the input box scrolls into focus.
- **Type or record a reply** → the text appears live, or the audio waveform animates as the child speaks. There is no "minimum length" gate.
- **Submit** → both the emoji choice and the reply are bundled and posted; the screen advances with a gentle thank-you note.
- **Idle** → the screen waits. There is no timer here. Reflection isn't a race.

## Shape of the experience
**1 round, deliberately.** This is not a game with progression — it is the single closing beat after a longer activity. The whole interaction takes 20–60 seconds. If a teacher wants reflection across multiple activities, they chain several of these screens with different prompts (*"How did you find the warm-up?"*, *"How did the puzzle feel?"*, *"What about the speed round?"*). Each screen is its own self-contained moment.

## Win condition and stars
There is no right answer and no wrong answer, so there are also no traditional stars or lives. Completion is **submission of an emoji choice and at least one character of text or one second of audio**. The star icon in the top-right shows `0/10` only because the screen sits inside a worksheet that uses a shared header — the screen itself does not award or deduct stars. A child who taps an emoji and writes nothing is gently nudged with *"Add a sentence so we can hear you."*; a child who only types but skips the emoji is nudged the other way. Both are required.

## Feel and motivation
- **Captions, not just faces.** The emojis are universally recognisable, but the captions remove ambiguity — there is no risk of a child tapping "sad face" because they thought it meant "I tried hard."
- **Quiet, not cute.** No confetti, no sound effects on submit, no animated mascot saying *"thanks for sharing!"* The screen is calm on purpose; loud feedback at this moment teaches kids that reflection is performative.
- **Voice for the youngest and the shyest.** Class 2 students often can't type fast enough to capture a thought before they lose it. The mic icon is the same size as the keyboard icon, signalling that speaking and typing are equally valid here.

## Why it works pedagogically
Most worksheets ask kids to do math and then move on. This screen makes the implicit question *"how did that feel for you?"* explicit, structured, and low-effort, which is the only way it gets answered honestly by a seven-year-old. Over weeks, the data this screen produces is a far more reliable signal of whether a topic is sticking than any test score: a child who keeps tapping *"I needed more help"* after fractions worksheets is telling the teacher exactly where the curriculum is brittle, before the next assessment makes it visible. The four-option range is a deliberate trade-off — five would invite a "neutral" middle choice, three would force false positives, four with a clear *tough-but-good* slot captures the real shape of how learning feels.
