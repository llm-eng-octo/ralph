# Friendly Pairs — The Drag-and-Sum Sorter

## In one line
A two-stage addition puzzle where the student first drags six numbers into rows that pair up to friendly totals (30, 40, 50), and only *after* the table is correctly arranged types in the grand total — turning a messy six-number sum into a tidy mental-math drill.

## Who it's for
Class 3–4 students (ages ~8–10) who can add two-digit numbers but still grind through `9 + 45 + 34 + 6 + 21 + 5` left-to-right with a finger and a pencil. They've heard the phrase *"make it ten"* but haven't yet generalised it to *"make it thirty"* or *"make it fifty"*. The game is for the in-between phase where students know addition but don't yet *strategise* addition.

## What it tries to teach
The whole game is built around one quietly powerful skill: **finding number pairs that sum to a clean target before adding the whole list.**
Three threads inside that:
- **Spotting complements.** Given `9` and `21` and `45`, the student needs to feel that `9 + 21 = 30` is clean — and that `9 + 45 = 54` isn't. The pairing table forces them to scan for partners, not just add neighbours.
- **Decomposing a list into chunks.** Six numbers added straight is hard to hold in working memory. Three pairs of clean tens is easy: `30 + 50 + 40 = 120`. The game teaches this *re-shape before sum* move explicitly.
- **Self-checking the answer.** Because the pairs are constrained to nice totals, a student who arranges them correctly *knows* the final sum is just three round numbers added — they can verify their input rather than guess.

## What the player sees and does
A clean white panel. At the top, the usual thin status row — round badge on the left, hearts and star tally on the right. Below that, a friendly story-style instruction: *"Rahul is trying to find the sum of the following numbers"* with the expression `9 + 45 + 34 + 6 + 21 + 5` shown in big bold type. A second paragraph explains: *"He arranged them as friendly pairs that make the sum 30, 40, or 50 in the table, but he has not done it correctly. Arrange the pairs in the correct order and then find the sum of all numbers."*

The middle of the screen is a 3-row, 2-column table with a `+` between the two cells of each row. Each cell holds one of the six numbers, but in the *wrong* arrangement (e.g. row 1 is `34 + 9`, which sums to 43 — neither 30, 40, nor 50). The student drags numbers between cells to fix the pairing.

At the bottom, an `Enter sum here →` label sits next to a small input box.

- **Drop a number into a pair that sums to a target (30/40/50)** → the row outline turns green and locks; that pair can no longer be disturbed.
- **Drop a number into a row whose pair doesn't yet hit a target** → the number rests in place, no penalty, but the row outline stays neutral. The student keeps trying.
- **All three rows green** → the input field at the bottom activates and gently pulses, inviting the typed total.
- **Type the correct grand sum and submit** → green tick, chime, round resolves.
- **Type a wrong sum** → the input shakes red, a heart shatters, the input clears for another try. The pairs stay locked — the student doesn't have to redo the dragging.

## Shape of the experience
10 rounds, each with a fresh six-number list:

- **Rounds 1–3 — Two targets only.** Lists pair into 30s and 40s. Numbers stay under 30. Students learn the rhythm of *scan for the partner, lock the row*.
- **Rounds 4–7 — Three targets, larger numbers.** Lists pair into 30s, 40s, and 50s. Numbers can be up to 49. The pairing search gets harder but the totals are still round.
- **Rounds 8–10 — Stretch.** Four pairs instead of three (eight numbers), and one pair sums to 60. The grand total can climb above 200. Students who learned the strategy fly; students still adding left-to-right struggle.

## Win condition and stars
Three lives across the whole session. Stars are awarded on the final tally:
- **3 stars** — finish all 10 rounds with at least 27 first-attempt correct (across 30 step-decisions: arrangements + sums).
- **2 stars** — 18–26 first-attempt correct.
- **1 star** — finish all 10 rounds with fewer than 18 first-attempt correct.
- **0 stars** — all three hearts lost before round 10.

## Feel and motivation
- **Pairs lock visibly.** When a row turns green and locks, the student gets a satisfying *click-it-into-place* feel — like a jigsaw piece. Three pieces, three locks, then the sum.
- **No penalty for exploring.** The drag is forgiving: numbers can be moved any number of times until a row is correct. The penalty is only on a wrong typed sum, because *that's* the moment the student actually claims to know the answer.
- **Targets are visible.** The instruction reminds them: 30, 40, 50. So the student isn't guessing what counts as "friendly" — they're hunting for specific sums.

## Why it works pedagogically
The mental-math instinct of *grouping before adding* is the gateway between primary arithmetic and useful, fluent number sense. Curricula often teach this strategy as a tip ("make tens!") but rarely give kids enough practice to internalise it. By forcing the student to *physically rearrange* numbers into clean pairs before the grand sum is even askable, this game makes the strategy unavoidable. After ten rounds, the student is no longer adding `9 + 45 + 34 + 6 + 21 + 5` left-to-right — they're seeing `(9 + 21) + (45 + 5) + (34 + 6) = 30 + 50 + 40` automatically.

A 10-round session runs about 8–10 minutes — long enough to settle into the rhythm, short enough that the strategy is still sharp at the end.
