# Two Boxes — The Factor Sorting Game

## In one line
A drag-and-drop sorting game where the student is handed a tray of numbers and two labelled boxes — *Factors of 20* and *Factors of 30* — and has to slot every number into the right box (or both boxes, when a number is a common factor) to feel for the first time what *common factor* really means.

## Who it's for
Class 4–5 students (ages ~9–11) who can recite times tables but freeze when asked *"Is 6 a factor of 30?"* — they know `5 × 6 = 30` but don't yet automatically reverse the fact into divisibility language. The game is for the student standing at that exact threshold: they have the multiplication facts, they don't yet have the *factor instinct*, and they are about to meet the idea of *common factor* for the first time.

## What it tries to teach
The whole game is built around one foundation skill: **recognising whether a small number divides a target number evenly**, repeated across many targets until the student feels the pattern.
Three threads inside that:
- **Factor as divisor.** A factor of 20 is *any* number that divides 20 with no remainder — including 1 and 20 themselves. Students who learn factors only as "numbers in the times table" miss the endpoints; this game catches that with `1` and `20` always present in the pool.
- **A number can belong to both.** The breakthrough moment of the game is the first time a student realises some numbers (1, 2, 5, 10) belong in *both* boxes. That is the literal definition of a *common factor*, and the student discovers it by hand instead of being told.
- **Eliminating non-factors.** Numbers like 3, 4, 15 (in the 20/30 case) are factors of one but not the other; numbers like 7 are factors of neither. Students learn to test each candidate against each target, not just one.

## What the player sees and does
A clean white panel. The status row at the top — a small avatar, the round badge ("Q1"), a heart counter (`2`) on the right, and the rolling star tally (`0/10`) with a yellow star.

Below the header, the instruction line: *"Drag and drop the numbers in the correct box."* — a small downward-pointing finger emoji to anchor the eye.

The body is a clean two-zone layout:

- **Top half — two target boxes.** Side by side, each labelled in dark text: **Factors of 20** and **Factors of 30**. Each box is a roomy white rectangle, big enough to hold five or six number tiles when full. The boxes share a soft grey background panel to read as a single answer area.

- **Bottom half — the number pool.** A larger soft-grey panel below holds a 4-column grid of draggable number tiles: in the canonical example, `1`, `2`, `3`, `4`, `5`, `6`, `10`, `15`, `20`, `30`. Each tile is a small white rounded square with the number in dark text. Tiles arrange themselves neatly when one is removed.

The interaction is direct: **press, drag, drop**. There is no preview ghost, no rotation, no tap-to-place — just lift and place. Some games in this family allow a single number tile to be dropped *twice* (once in each box) for common factors; the canonical version offers two copies of those numbers in the pool so each box gets its own physical tile. The student feels the duplication.

- **Drop a number into the right box** → the tile snaps into place, a small chime plays, the tile fades from the pool.
- **Drop a number into the wrong box** → the box flashes red briefly, the tile bounces back to the pool, a one-line nudge: *"15 doesn't divide 20 evenly — it goes 1 time with 5 left over. Try the other box."*
- **Drop a non-factor into either box** → both boxes flash red briefly, the tile returns to the pool: *"7 doesn't divide 20 or 30 evenly — leave it in the pool... wait, all numbers must be sorted!"* (Late rounds remove this safety: non-factors must be left aside.)
- **Three wrong drops in a single round** → a heart shatters, the round ends.

## Shape of the experience
10 rounds across three pockets:

- **Rounds 1–3 — One box, no decoys.** Just *Factors of 12* with a pool of six numbers (1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12). Students learn the mechanic and feel what *factor* means without the dual-box pressure.
- **Rounds 4–7 — Two boxes, all numbers belong somewhere.** *Factors of 20* and *Factors of 30* with a pool of ten numbers, every one a factor of at least one target. Common factors duplicate. This is the heart of the game.
- **Rounds 8–10 — Two boxes, with non-factors.** *Factors of 18* and *Factors of 24* with a pool of twelve numbers, two of which (e.g. 5, 7) are factors of *neither* — the student must leave them in the pool. Round complete only when every *factor* is sorted and every *non-factor* is left behind. This is where the skill becomes real.

Three hearts carry across the whole session. There is no timer.

## Win condition and stars
Stars are awarded on completion of each round, then summed:
- **3 stars** — round completed with zero wrong drops.
- **2 stars** — round completed with 1–2 wrong drops.
- **1 star** — round completed with 3+ wrong drops (and at least one heart still alive).
- **0 stars** — round abandoned via heart loss.

A perfect run is 30 stars across 10 rounds.

## Feel and motivation
- **The two-box layout teaches by parallelism.** Putting the *Factors of 20* box right next to the *Factors of 30* box invites a side-by-side comparison the moment the student notices that the same number (1, 2, 5, 10) fits both. That comparison is the foundation of the *Highest Common Factor* lesson coming next year — and this game plants the seed without naming it.
- **The pool is hand-sized.** Ten tiles is enough to feel like a real sorting task and few enough that the student can hold the whole pool in their visual field. No scrolling, no second page.
- **Snap-back is gentle.** Wrong drops bounce, never destroy. The whole tactile loop — pick up, hover, place — is designed to feel like sorting laundry, not taking a quiz.

## Why it works pedagogically
The factor concept is one of the highest-leverage ideas in middle-school arithmetic — it underpins fractions (simplifying), divisibility, primes, LCM, GCD, and most number-theoretic intuition. But it's introduced in textbooks as a definition (*"a number that divides another with no remainder"*) before students have any felt sense of *which* numbers tend to be factors. This game inverts that: students sort their way to the definition. By the end of round 7, a student who started by guessing has usually noticed that "even numbers are usually factors of even targets" and "odd targets don't take even factors" — they're rediscovering basic divisibility rules through play. A 10-round session takes 6–8 minutes; running it three times across a week measurably improves a student's speed on factor-list questions in the rest of their work.
