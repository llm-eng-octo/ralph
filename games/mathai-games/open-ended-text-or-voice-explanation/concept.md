# Faster Way — The Strategy-Naming Reflection

## In one line
A reflection prompt where, after grinding through a slow one-by-one method, the student is asked — in their own words, by typing or speaking — *what would be a faster way?* and articulates the named strategy (division, repeated subtraction, grouping) that the curriculum is about to introduce.

## Who it's for
Class 4–5 students (ages ~9–11) who have just spent a few questions distributing items by counting one at a time (108 pins to 4 students, one at a time, until they ran out of pins or patience). They've felt the pain of the slow method. They might know the word "division" but haven't connected it to *this* situation yet. The game is for the moment when a student is *ripe* to invent the next idea — and needs only a prompt to put words to it.

## What it tries to teach
The whole game is built around one meta-skill: **noticing when a method is inefficient and naming a better one.**
Three threads inside that:
- **Method awareness.** Most students never get asked *was that method fast?* They just do the next problem. This game pauses the worksheet and forces them to look at what they just did and judge it.
- **Articulating in their own words.** The input is multi-modal — text *or* voice — and intentionally open-ended. There is no "select the correct method from these four options". The student must produce language: *"I would divide 108 by 4"* or *"I would put them in groups of 4 and count the groups"*.
- **Connecting to a named operation.** The grading is generous — any answer that names division, equal grouping, or repeated subtraction counts. The point is not vocabulary; it's that the student notices "the slow thing I just did has a name, and that name is something I already half-know."

## What the player sees and does
A clean white panel. At the very top, the usual thin status row — round badge, hearts, star tally. Below that, the prompt builds up over three short paragraphs:

1. *"You tried to give away **108 pins to 4 students.**"* (Sets the scene, recalling the previous activity.)
2. *"We saw that giving out one pin at a time to find the number of pins each student gets and the number of pins left is a slow method."* (Names the slow method explicitly so the student knows what they're being asked to improve.)
3. *"How can you find the number of pins each student gets and the number of pins left in a **faster way**?"* (The actual question.)

Below the text, a wide grey input panel sits — taller than typical text inputs, signalling *we expect a few sentences, not a single number*. Inside it, two icons sit at the bottom: a 🎤 microphone for voice input, a ⌨️ keyboard for text input, and a ↩ undo arrow on the right. Either input mode is fine — the student picks.

- **Type or speak any answer that names a faster strategy** → the input glows green, a soft chime, an animation acknowledges *good idea — let's try it on the next screen*. The round resolves and the game continues.
- **Type or speak something off-topic, or stay silent** → no harsh penalty. The game gently re-prompts with a hint: *"Think about what you'd do if you had to give 108 sweets to 4 friends — what's a faster way than one-by-one?"*. No heart loss until two consecutive non-answers.
- **Type something half-right, like "I would count in 4s"** → the game accepts it (repeated counting *is* a step toward division), gives a small green tick with an encouraging note: *"yes — and there's a name for that. it's called **division**."*

## Shape of the experience
This archetype is **a single round**, but it sits inside a larger lesson sequence — usually as round N of a longer worksheet where the previous N-1 rounds were the slow method. The expansion to 5–10 rounds for a standalone game would loop the same structure across different scenarios:

- **Round 1 — Sharing pins (division).** 108 pins, 4 students. Student names division.
- **Round 2 — Cutting cake (fractions).** A cake into 8 equal pieces by trial-and-error, then *"is there a faster way to know exactly where to cut?"*
- **Round 3 — Counting books (multiplication).** 7 shelves of 12 books each, counted one by one, then *"what's faster?"*
- **Round 4 — Splitting time (rate).** Walking 30km in 5 hours, asked one km at a time, then *"how would you find the speed?"*
- **Round 5 — Stretch.** A scenario where the "faster way" is a less obvious operation (subtraction-as-comparison, or grouping-as-multiplication).

Each round mirrors the same shape: do the slow thing → realise it's slow → name the strategy.

## Win condition and stars
This is reflection, not arithmetic, so the lives system is light. Three lives, but a heart only fires after *two* consecutive non-answers in a single round.
- **3 stars** — answer all rounds with a clearly named strategy on the first attempt.
- **2 stars** — answer all rounds, with at least one needing a hint nudge.
- **1 star** — answer all rounds eventually.
- **0 stars** — abandon before round 5.

The grading is intentionally lenient because the *act of articulating* is the goal, not the precision of the term used.

## Feel and motivation
- **Empty space invites writing.** The input panel is large and bottom-anchored, signalling *we want a real answer*. Compared to a tiny one-line input, this layout shifts the student into "explanation mode" rather than "answer mode".
- **Voice as a first-class input.** A microphone button alongside the keyboard means students who can articulate verbally but not yet write fluently can still play. This is genuinely inclusive for younger or dyslexic students.
- **No "right answer" tile pre-coded.** The game uses LLM-style flexible matching for the response — accepting any phrasing that captures the strategy. This avoids the rigid trap of "type 'division' exactly" that plagues other open-ended worksheets.

## Why it works pedagogically
There's a particular cognitive event that maths education tries to engineer: the moment when a student, mid-task, *invents* the next operation because they're tired of the slow one. Teachers call this *desirable difficulty* — the friction is what makes the lesson stick. This game ritualises that moment: the student does the slow thing, feels the slow, *names* the faster thing in their own words, and then the next lesson teaches the formal version of that named thing. By the time the textbook says *"today we're learning division,"* the student is already nodding — *yes, that's what I was about to invent.* That sense of ownership is worth more than any worksheet score.

A 5-round reflection sequence takes 4–7 minutes — long enough to genuinely think, short enough that students don't drift into joke answers.
