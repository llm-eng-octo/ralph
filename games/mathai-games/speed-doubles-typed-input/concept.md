# Party Math — The Equal-Groups Story Trainer

## In one line
A friendly word-problem game where Ravi (and friends) keep planning birthday parties — *"3 pencils each for 4 friends, how many pencils?"* — and the student types the total, learning to translate "each" into multiplication without the multiplication sign ever scaring them off.

## Who it's for
Class 2–3 students (ages ~7–9) who can comfortably skip-count by 2s, 5s, and 10s but freeze when a story problem says "each." The game is for the in-between moment where repeated addition (`3 + 3 + 3 + 3`) is solid but the student hasn't yet seen that *that's the same as `3 × 4`*. It targets the specific stuck-point where a child reads "3 pencils to each of 4 friends" and has no idea whether to add, subtract, multiply, or guess.

## What it tries to teach
The whole game is built around one quiet skill: **recognizing equal-groups stories and turning them into a single number**, whether by repeated addition or by multiplication.
Three threads inside that:
- **Spotting "each".** The keyword the game leans on hardest is *each*. "3 pencils for **each** friend." "5 sweets in **each** packet." "2 wheels on **each** car." Once a student trains their ear to that word, they have a reliable signal for *"this is a times-problem, not an add-problem."*
- **Group-size vs. number-of-groups.** Most early multiplication errors come from confusing the two numbers in the story. The game is careful: every story names a group size (3 pencils) and a number of groups (4 friends), and the right answer is always group size × number of groups. Decoys deliberately include `3 + 4 = 7` and `4 - 3 = 1` to catch students reaching for the wrong operation.
- **Building toward times-tables.** The numbers stay small and friendly — group sizes from 2 to 5, number of groups from 2 to 6 — so the answers all live inside the times-tables a student is already learning. Every solved round is silent rehearsal of a fact like `3 × 4 = 12`.

## What the player sees and does
A clean white panel, gentle and uncluttered. At the top a thin status row — Q1 badge with a friendly avatar, a heart icon with `2` lives remaining, and `0/10` stars on the right.

The body of the screen reads like a picture book. First a sentence in large soft text: *"Ravi is planning a birthday party and wants to give **3 pencils** to each friend who comes to the party."* The bold words pull the eye to the two key numbers.

Below the sentence, a cheerful illustration: a cartoon boy in a red shirt waving, holding a fistful of yellow pencils. The illustration is the whole middle of the screen — almost a hero panel — making the story feel less like math and more like a children's book.

Underneath the illustration, the question: *"How many pencils will he need if **4 friends** come to the party?"* The number-of-friends is the second bold word.

At the bottom, a single white input box with the unit *"pencils"* labelled to its right. There is no number pad on screen — the student types using the device keyboard.

- **Type the correct total and submit** → the box turns green, the pencils in Ravi's hand multiply briefly with a soft *pop*, the round badge ticks up, and a star is awarded.
- **Type a wrong total** → the box shakes red, a heart shatters in the top right, and a one-line hint appears: *"3 pencils for each of 4 friends. Try counting: 3, 6, 9, 12."* The correct answer reveals after a beat.
- **Idle for 20 seconds** → a soft glow surrounds the two bold numbers in the story, nudging the student to multiply them.

## Shape of the experience
10 rounds, with the story shifting but the structure staying constant:

- **Rounds 1–3 — Tiny groups.** Group sizes of 2 or 3, group counts of 2 or 3. Stories like *"2 balloons for each of 3 cousins"* or *"3 sweets for each of 2 brothers."* Answers stay under 10. The student gets the rhythm.
- **Rounds 4–7 — Times-table territory.** Group sizes 2–5, group counts 3–6. Answers in the 6–25 range. New characters and new objects each round (Priya stacks 4 books on each of 5 shelves; Aman puts 5 chocolates in each of 4 boxes).
- **Rounds 8–10 — Stretch.** Group sizes up to 6, group counts up to 6. Answers up to 36. The student is now solidly in `× 4`, `× 5`, `× 6` territory, and the time pressure is gentler — the game wants the student to *think it through*, not race.

Lives carry across rounds — three hearts total — and the game ends when either all 10 rounds are complete or all 3 hearts are lost.

## Win condition and stars
Stars are awarded **per round**:
- **3 stars** — correct on the first try, no idle hint shown.
- **2 stars** — correct on the first try, but the idle hint glow appeared.
- **1 star** — correct eventually, after a wrong attempt.
- **0 stars** — round abandoned (only possible if all hearts are lost).

A perfect run is 30 stars across 10 rounds, displayed as *"X / 10"* in the top corner with the harder rounds weighted higher.

## Feel and motivation
- **Stories feel like real birthday-party planning.** Names are common (Ravi, Priya, Aman, Sara), objects are concrete (pencils, sweets, balloons, books), and every story is a tiny logistical puzzle: someone is preparing for an event and needs to know how many of *something* to buy. The math lives inside a small social purpose.
- **The illustration carries the count.** When the student types the correct answer, Ravi's bunch of pencils briefly multiplies and animates into the right total — the visual confirms the count. This makes the connection between *the number 12* and *twelve actual pencils* explicit.
- **Decoys teach.** When a child types `7` for `3 × 4` (i.e. they added), the hint specifically says *"3 + 4 is 7, but each friend needs 3 pencils — so it's 3 four times. Try counting: 3, 6, 9, 12."* When a child types `12` for `3 × 4` they're right; when they type `1` (i.e. they subtracted) the hint addresses subtraction directly. The wrong answers are diagnostic, and the hints target the specific misread.

## Why it works pedagogically
"Each" is one of the smallest, sneakiest words in elementary math. A whole curriculum's worth of word-problem competence rides on whether a student parses it correctly when they read it. Most textbooks introduce multiplication as a symbol (`×`) before they introduce the *language* that maps to it, which means students learn the operation in the abstract and then get blindsided by stories that never use the symbol. This game inverts that: it lives entirely inside the language, so the student's ear is trained on "each" and "every" and "per" before they ever see `3 × 4 = 12` written down. After two weeks of daily play, the student can read a multiplication word problem and *hear* the multiplication in it — which is the actual skill that unlocks the next two years of arithmetic.

A 10-round session takes about 4–6 minutes. Short enough to fit before snack time, long enough to feel like a real practice block.
